Chapter 7 Skeuomorphism

Reality

Like it or not, we live in a physical world.

We observe and interact through our senses. We read books, magazines, and newspapers. We twist door knobs, open and close cabinets, organize shelves, tie knots. We sleep, we sit, we walk, we eat.

In each of these examples, we're interacting with an object through its interface. While many of us spend eight or more hours a day facing a screen, we all grew up in the physical world, and interact with it regularly. The sum of these interactions make up our sense of reality.

Screens

Until we invent a better interface for presenting software, screens are our canvas. A typical screen is flat, emits light, and sits on a surface. Whether they're big or small, backlit or projected, curved or flat, screens are screens.

They exist in reality, but they cannot provide a one-to-one mapping for each interaction reality has to offer.

Real-world metaphors

The principle of least astonishment [#] tells us that when designing an interface, we should borrow from functionally similar interfaces to minimize the learning curve.

Sometimes this means borrowing patterns from other software. But since humans and the world and all of our learned behavior came before computers, usually this means borrwing from the real world.
