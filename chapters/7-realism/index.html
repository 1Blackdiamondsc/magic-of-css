Chapter 7 Realism

Like it or not, we live in a physical world.

We observe and interact through our senses. We read books, magazines, and newspapers. We open doors, turn on faucets, open and close cabinets, tie knots. We interact with each real-world object through its interface.

Screens are objects in the real world, but they present to us a separate universe of metaphorical objects. Why is this any different? Because—at least for the moment—software is displayed on screens which are literally flat.

The cost of realism

CSS finally has the power to do almost everything realism-proponents have wanted for years.

etc.

We have shadows, gradients, masks, opacity, layers, rotations, skew, etc. etc., transitions, easing functions. These tools can be used to simulate real world lighting and geometries, real-world physics such as accelerations, gravity, spring forces, waves, etc. etc. And if you have a need for such tools, by all means.

But the true cost of realism comes in one of two forms (two sides of the same coin really): 1) the moment you can't do something in the screen world that you have to to complete a metaphor you've started, or 2) the moment you wish to break the metaphor for the purpose of achieving someting in the screen world which is trivial, but to accept it means you must lose the metaphor.

Let's look at examples of each of these two costs.
